Wheely


Wheely is a B2C e-commerce website that is designed for wheelchair users to make their purchase decision more carefully using unique features.

This is a design project that I worked on to deliver a web-based solution that bridges the gap between wheelchair users, business owners, and occupational therapists, to facilitate a collaborative custom wheelchair buying experience.

INTERNET FINDINGS


After searching on internet (ex: Twitter, Google, Facebook) I found out some initial user-centered pain points and potential areas of improvements such as:

  • There can be up to 200 measurements taken for a custom wheelchair, and a lot of 'eyeballing' involved. This results in inconsistencies secondary to input variability and error, ultimately leading to user uncertainty regarding custom fitting.
  • Scripting process can be intrusive and awkward for the user.
  • Wheelchair manufacture process is not interactive and the user often does not know what stage of development or when their wheelchair will be complete.
  • User does not have instant feedback and have no way of knowing how the chair will feel and look like.
  • Lack of centralised and digitised data collection process.

PROBLEM HYPOTHESIS


With the above information, I formed the following problem hypothesis:

Wheelchair users feel uncertain during the scripting process and want better control and transparency over their entire journey, however current technology and the environment does not provide for it.

DISCOVERY PHASE


To solve the problem, I need to understand the existing process!

With the above problem hypothesis and limited insights into the wheelchair industry, I kick off the discovery phase with these research objectives:

  • A better understanding of the current end-to-end process of procuring a custom wheelchair from the perspective of the wheelchair user, scripters (measurer) and business owners.
  • Exploring user goals and pain points in the above experience and journey.


The pandemic has greatly affected the way we interact, particularly in-store purchasing. With the various lockdowns and restricted access for users to view products and get in-person measurements, exploring and understanding the behaviours and preferences of online shoppers, particularly made-to-order products, was crucial.

KEY INSIGHTS

The process is daunting, particularly for new users.

There is a general lack of information and direction given to user when trying to procure a custom wheelchair. On top of that, custom wheelchairs are costly in USA ($6k–12k), often requiring lengthly NDIS funding which can take up to 12 months for approval.
There is limited visibility on the timeline, approvals and progress.>

Resulting in a lot of manual follow up by users through phone calls and emails.
Creating a best-fit custom wheelchair needs to be a collaborative process.

Requiring the input of the Occupational Therapists (scripters), wheelchair users, wheelchair business owners and NDIS, which the industry does not currently facilitate communications between. Great therapists will ask lifestyle questions, not just take measurements.
Business owners and scripters are busy and time-poor.

Resulting in lack of communication and information provided to users to keep them in the loop.
Trialing chairs is an essential step in the process.

It is extremely useful for users to tailor their needs to the type of wheelchair they are looking for, a current design step missing in the procurement process.

"You don't know what you need and you rely on other people. I found myself in a world of other people in wheelchairs and their degrees of function, and based on that degree of function, what sort of needs and supports and even accessories...I'd need."

Reflecting on their experience as a new wheelchair user figuring out a customised chair for their specific needs

REDEFINING THE PROBLEM

Scripting process was not the key problem


From the collated research and subsequent personas, I got to know that the original problem hypothesis was not the problem users were experiencing.

I Realigned and redefined the problems to reach a more relevant solution. The following questions were developed to help narrow the scope of the solution.

PROBLEM STATEMENT

  1. “HMW provide information and transparency so that wheelchair users have more confidence and control over their experience?”

  2. “HMW reduce manual input and streamline processes so that business owners (including scripters) have more time to help more people?”



Generating idea concepts


I did an ideation process to generate potential solutions to the above guiding questions and tackled the user pain points that I found on the overall research process. Then prioritized a few based on how much value they add to the user and feasibility (time and money).

Techniques used included brainwriting, hand drawn + lo-fi wireframe, MVP matrix.

USABILIY TEST

Putting the design to the test with a selected user group


I conducted usability testing with 3 participants, including wheelchair users and occupational therapists. The goal is to ensure the design was clear, the flows easily followed, and to validate what I’ve created actually meets users' needs.

The overall feedback were extremely positive which gave me confidence that the concepts I had created were hitting the mark. Changes were still apparent, but for the majority focused on terminology and content placement to ensure a seamless experience.

"Great concept and will make it easier when dealing with my OT and the NDIS"

"Seeing the progress of my build in my account is an awesome feature."

"Really liked it, simplified the process and helped my understanding of what would be good for me. Narrowing down options was extremely helpful."

HOME PAGE

High-Quality-Prototype

Personable and approachable

As a first point of call, users are invited to try the Wheelchair guide feature to discover a wheelchair that is right for them.

Results from the research revealed that 43% of online shoppers find videos extremely helpful in decision-making when buying made-to-order products online.

As such, features such as explainer videos of the process and reviews and testimonials from existing clients were included to provide potential users with as much information and transparency as possible, enabling them to make more informed purchasing decision.

Pain points tackled:
✅ Information and transparency
✅ Confidence and control

WHEELCHAIR GUIDE

High-Quality-Prototype
A new feature which includes high-level questions to help users narrow down their options and give them control and transparency with what's available.

Features:

  • High-level questions as a starting point to help users find suitable wheelchair options
  • Percentage wheel to keep users inform of the progress.
  • Information button for further clarification along the way.
  • Ability to skip questions if users are unsure.
  • View wheelchair matches and their key benefits. User can then customise, view more details, email their matches or save to their account.

Pain points tackled:
✅ Daunting process
✅ Information and direction

PRODUCT PAGE

High-Quality-Prototype
Research shows that users prefer to do their own research beforehand. They want the ability to view product options available before approaching someone. The 3D Customiser provide users with a visual context in the digital space, replicating an in-person experience with the freedom to browse commitment-free.

Features on Product page:

  • 360° preview of wheelchair models
  • Product filter is perfect for experienced wheelchair users who knows what they want and does not need to use the Wheelchair Guide.
  • Product details for in-depth information of the selected wheelchair, which includes promotional trailer, insights and recommendations from healthcare therapists
  • Reviews of the product from other wheelchair users

Features on 3D Customiser:

  • Live 360° preview of wheelchair with ability to zoom to specific parts
  • Percentage wheel and secondary menu to keep users informed of the progress of the customisation.
  • Item details of accessories and add-ons.
  • Summary and estimated quote of the selected items.
  • Ability to collaborate with and contact the healthcare therapist through a build code.

Pain points tackled:
✅ Control and transparency
✅ Instant feedback

TRACK MY PROCESS

High-Quality-Prototype

A feature which allows users to track their wheelchair progress, which is not limited to, but includes tracking of NDIS funding. This improves transparency for users and simultaneously reduced business owner's time spent on manual updates and phone calls.

Features


  • Status updates through email and sms subscriptions to keep users in the loop even without logging in.
  • Access helpful information like main point of contact, documentations and other details in one place.
  • Photo updates of wheelchair being constructed and parts arriving, to keep users involved in the process.
  • All message correspondence with Wheely and user's therapist easily accessible.
  • Draft of wheelchairs for ones that were incomplete.

Pain points tackled:
✅ Reduce excessive back-and-forth communication
✅ Transparency
✅ Involvement and in control

VISUAL COLLABORATION

High-Quality-Prototype

A collaboration portal where healthcare therapists can manage and provide helpful information to their clients, and collaborate with wheely to design and build a wheelchair that is unique to the user.

Features


  • Client list where the healthcare therapist can manage and recommend wheelchair to.their customers.
  • Add new client and enter their scripting form to their account
  • Recommend wheelchair, a feature for healthcare therapists to generate and share a unique code to the user for virtual access.
  • Draft of wheelchairs for ones that were incomplete.

Pain points tackled:
✅ Collaboration
✅ Communication
✅ Centralised data collection

LEARNINGS AND TAKEAWAYS


  • Understanding that the problem space is fluid and prone to change depending on the research.
  • Research can be very handy and useful when they’re done correctly with proper thoughts.
  • Thinking product constraints are as important as user constrains.
  • 3D features are really useful for the e-commerce industry.
  • Some small features can have big effects on user engagement.
  • Be comfortable with the 'unknown': understanding when to think far and wide without limitation and when to narrow down and focus.
  • Don't focus too much on the details early on: it's difficult to show and test a product that does not look 'polish', but acknowledging that design is an iterative process that needs to be tested and validated.
